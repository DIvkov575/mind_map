- generalized function that grow (at most) polynomially at infinity and are continuous linear functionals on the **Schwartz space**.

- Tempered space is the [[Dual Space]] of [[Schwartz Space]]
- Forms a proper subspace of (the space of distributions on R^n)
- All tempered distributions (generalized functions) have [[Fourier transform]]

- Tempered distributions are **generalized functions** that allow singularities (like the Dirac delta) and functions that grow polynomially.
- They are exactly the distributions for which **Fourier transforms are always defined**
- The **significance of a tempered distribution** is that it lets you **rigorously handle “generalized functions” that grow at most polynomially**, while still allowing all the operations that classical analysis and Fourier analysis rely on.


Defined below are the [[Schwartz space]] ![{\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0078d18e4675b6e7e2acb6c2c25c65294193e36d) and its [[Dual Space]]; the space of **_tempered distributions_** ![{\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}'(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78ca4a0bc72d2677618a39482625e3f749990455), which forms a proper subspace of ![{\displaystyle {\mathcal {D}}'(\mathbb {R} ^{n});}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf6386713019254a2b1eddbd5780769f4e41a58b) the space of distributions on ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76ef548febfc9981762740107858be9e3a5576c3) Tempered distributions are useful if one studies the Fourier transform since all tempered distributions have a Fourier transform, which is not true for an arbitrary distribution in D′(Rn).![{\displaystyle {\mathcal {D}}'(\mathbb {R} ^{n}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe36ec641e10f4010ad5d107992f5c7631ec5880)

**Tempered Distributions**
The [[inclusion map]] ![{\displaystyle \operatorname {In} :{\mathcal {D}}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})\to {\mathcal {S}}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/441d95bd1054f01e9921363464dca0a25833e318) is a continuous injection whose image is [[dense]] in its codomain, so the transpose ![{\displaystyle {}^{\text{t}}\!\operatorname {In} :({\mathcal {S}}(\mathbb {R} ^{n}))'_{b}\to {\mathcal {D}}'(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9899f49491003f85548b72cc2f14ab38ec7a5556) is also a continuous injection. Thus, the image of the transpose map, denoted by ![{\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}'(\mathbb {R} ^{n}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8607acc2937079f3f46703f6af708a0074ad510) forms a space of distributions.

The space S′(Rn)![{\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}'(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78ca4a0bc72d2677618a39482625e3f749990455) is called the space of _tempered distributions_. It is the [continuous dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_dual_space "Continuous dual space") of the Schwartz space. Equivalently, a distribution T![{\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is a tempered distribution if and only if ![{\displaystyle \left({\text{ for all }}\alpha ,\beta \in \mathbb {N} ^{n}:\lim _{m\to \infty }p_{\alpha ,\beta }(\phi _{m})=0\right)\Longrightarrow \lim _{m\to \infty }T(\phi _{m})=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99f4c9cceaf8144101c462e6639a3d2d5edad51a)

The derivative of a tempered distribution is again a tempered distribution. Tempered distributions generalize the bounded (or slow-growing) locally integrable functions; all distributions with compact support and all [square-integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable "Square-integrable") functions are tempered distributions. More generally, all functions that are products of polynomials with elements of the [_Lp_ space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space "Lp space") Lp(Rn)![{\displaystyle L^{p}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5cf52173a2565b7aa70dc34922de083d57bfbe) for p≥1![{\displaystyle p\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf93c1353080a21b276e79058d82c19c40310653) are tempered distributions.

The _tempered distributions_ can also be characterized as _slowly growing_, meaning that each derivative of T![{\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) grows at most as fast as some [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial"). This characterization is dual to the _rapidly falling_ behaviour of the derivatives of a function in the Schwartz space, where each derivative of ϕ![{\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) decays faster than every inverse power of |x|.![{\displaystyle |x|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b62265e223769ba94a6452c1ee279d3bcc574016) An example of a rapidly falling function is |x|nexp⁡(−λ|x|β)![{\displaystyle |x|^{n}\exp(-\lambda |x|^{\beta })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/306e6087c9f970ac5937cf0d92372201d1ab2aef) for any positive n,λ,β.![{\displaystyle n,\lambda ,\beta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ea09a03398312a4acb1d955a533a74b4bb37987)
