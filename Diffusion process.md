### Definition
A diffusion process is defined by the following properties. There is a unique family of probability measures Pa;bξ,τ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {P} _{a;b}^{\xi ,\tau }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65237848224e6c30cf276dade755a2f57b8c2f07) (for τ≥0![{\displaystyle \tau \geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3365b470c89debfe869cc61eaadb828cef75ba24), ξ∈Rd![{\displaystyle \xi \in \mathbb {R} ^{d}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c5cecc273db3daee1cbab006a4398b96dbccaa4)) on the canonical space Ω=C([0,∞),Rd)![{\displaystyle \Omega =C([0,\infty ),\mathbb {R} ^{d})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa6b7516caabb30ac3ce6050361a5faec7954393), with its Borel σ![{\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36)-algebra, such that:

## SDE Construction and Infinitesimal Generator

It is clear that if we have an La;b![{\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{a;b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df232c4cdccd7d239c49483f3850dfd0c2e7aeae)-diffusion, i.e. (Xt)t≥0![{\displaystyle (X_{t})_{t\geq 0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21296940fbeeae13b27893bdb77c0ae6a02ad23f) on (Ω,F,Ft,Pa;bξ,τ)![{\displaystyle (\Omega ,{\mathcal {F}},{\mathcal {F}}_{t},\mathbb {P} _{a;b}^{\xi ,\tau })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf706f5a500917c37a15370693e2f4bead3a4f88), then Xt![{\displaystyle X_{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82120d04dfb3cbadc4912951dd12b5568c9cd8f3) satisfies the SDE dXti=12∑k=1dσki(Xt)dBtk+bi(Xt)dt![{\displaystyle dX_{t}^{i}={\frac {1}{2}}\,\sum _{k=1}^{d}\sigma _{k}^{i}(X_{t})\,dB_{t}^{k}+b^{i}(X_{t})\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9081b857e1c853b2adc86ca23846a7a581bd72f). In contrast, one can construct this diffusion from that SDE if aij(x,t)=∑kσik(x,t)σjk(x,t)![{\displaystyle a^{ij}(x,t)=\sum _{k}\sigma _{i}^{k}(x,t)\,\sigma _{j}^{k}(x,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52108bf2d30d6d4cb93fb867d12efbd25aee9f52) and σij(x,t)![{\displaystyle \sigma ^{ij}(x,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb3319f06299312ea35fbf5ae8273945bf2a558d), bi(x,t)![{\displaystyle b^{i}(x,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97f511c570b8a11a1c6ab2aae899b9c057d2d0f3) are Lipschitz continuous. To see this, let Xt![{\displaystyle X_{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82120d04dfb3cbadc4912951dd12b5568c9cd8f3) solve the SDE starting at Xτ=ξ![{\displaystyle X_{\tau }=\xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30e281082498ebd35519b735f470397d6ee03f9). The law of Xt![{\displaystyle X_{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82120d04dfb3cbadc4912951dd12b5568c9cd8f3) defines Pa;bξ,τ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {P} _{a;b}^{\xi ,\tau }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65237848224e6c30cf276dade755a2f57b8c2f07) on Ω=C([0,∞),Rd)![{\displaystyle \Omega =C([0,\infty ),\mathbb {R} ^{d})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa6b7516caabb30ac3ce6050361a5faec7954393) with the correct initial condition and local martingale property.
